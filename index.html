The big idea

In CF, thick mucus slowly damages the pancreas, so it can't make enough insulin. Insulin is the key that lets the body's cells take in the energy from food. When there isn't enough, sugar from food stays in the blood instead of feeding the body. So the job is simple to say: put insulin in from outside to do the work the pancreas can't.

Why she can still eat the treats

This is the part that trips most people up, because it's the opposite of the usual "diabetes means cut the sugar" message. CF needs lots of calories to grow well and keep the lungs strong, so food is rarely the thing to restrict. The rule isn't "ban the carbs" — it's "cover the carbs with insulin." That's exactly why this tool counts carbs instead of cutting foods out.

What "carb counting" actually means

Carbohydrates (the sugars and starches in food) are the part that raises blood sugar. Protein and fat barely move it in the short term. So you add up the grams of carb in the meal — and that's the number this tool gives you at the bottom.

Turning carbs into a dose

Her team gives you a ratio — something like "1 unit of insulin per 10g of carb." The sum is just: meal carbs ÷ ratio = the mealtime dose. That's the ratio box in the tool. The tool does that division for you, but the ratio itself only ever comes from her team.

The words the doctors use

Bolus — the fast insulin you give to cover a meal or snack.
Basal — slow background insulin (some kids need it, some don't yet). It is not for food.
Carb ratio (ICR) — how many grams of carb one unit covers. The number in the tool.
Correction factor (ISF) — extra insulin to bring a high reading down, separate from food. The tool does not do this.
Hypo — blood sugar dropping too low. Treated with fast sugar, then re-check.

Why some foods are flagged "acts slower"

Chips, pizza, creamy pasta and ice cream are high in fat, so they raise blood sugar more slowly and for longer than the carb number alone suggests. If you see a low soon after and a high a few hours later with these meals, that's the fat at work — and a good thing to raise with the team, who may split or stretch the dose.

Timing

Fast insulin is usually given just before eating and works over a few hours. A reading won't move the moment you dose — rapid insulin takes a couple of hours to do most of its work.

Lows — hypoglycaemia ("hypo")

This is blood sugar dropping too low, usually under 4 mmol/L. It happens when there's more insulin on board than the food or activity needed. Signs in a child: shaky, sweaty, pale, suddenly hungry, irritable or teary, dizzy, or just "not right." It can come on fast and needs treating even if she feels okay.

The usual fix is the 15 / 15 rule: give about 15g of fast sugar, wait 15 minutes, then re-check — and repeat if she's still low. This is where the sweets and syrup lists double as a treatment tool, since you'll know the fast carbs to hand. Follow with a small snack so it doesn't dip again. A severe low (can't swallow, fitting, unconscious) is an emergency — that's what prescribed glucagon and an emergency call are for.

Highs — hyperglycaemia ("hyper")

This is blood sugar running too high, when there isn't enough insulin for the glucose in her blood. Symptoms usually start once she's above roughly 10–11 mmol/L: very thirsty, weeing a lot, tired, headache, sometimes blurry vision. Common causes are not enough insulin, more carbs than were covered, a missed dose, stress, or — very often in CF — illness or infection, which makes insulin work less well.

When a high is sustained, the body can start breaking down fat instead, which makes ketones. That's the thing to watch: check ketones when she's high or unwell, push water, and use the correction dose her team set (separate from meal carbs — this tool does not do it). Re-check after a couple of hours rather than re-dosing straight away.

Phone the team or get urgent care if: blood ketones are 1.5 mmol/L or higher, she's vomiting or has tummy pain, is drowsy or confused, is breathing fast or deep, or has fruity-smelling breath. These are the warning signs of ketoacidosis (DKA) and need care right away — don't wait it out.
The one rule to hold onto: this tool helps with the carb maths only. The ratio, any correction, and every change come from your CF and diabetes team. When something they say doesn't land, jot it down and ask again — and if you have a CGM, the patterns it shows make the "why" click far faster than any explanation.
